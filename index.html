// 02 — The Project

Sahara Ventus

Egypt's progress is impeded by several grand challenges. This project addresses pollution and improving the use of alternative energies. The problem to be solved is reducing pollution in Kharga Oasis, New Valley, where the local population depends entirely on diesel generators for electricity, which emit carbon dioxide, nitrogen oxides, sulfur oxides, and particulate matter.

Nitrogen oxides contribute to smog formation and respiratory disease; sulfur oxides produce acid rain that degrades soil and biodiversity; particulate matter penetrates lung tissue, causing cardiovascular illness. The purpose of this study is to design a renewable energy system to replace diesel generation as the community's primary power source.

A Savonius VAWT wind turbine was designed using locally available resources — aluminum and MDF wood — targeting a power output sufficient to exceed baseline energy demands. Requirements: generate a minimum of 150 Joules in 5 minutes, self-start at 4 m/s, withstand 60 °C without deformation, and stay within 0.25 m³ volume.

Results demonstrated that the system successfully exceeds all required parameters and operated reliably, offering a viable, clean alternative that directly reduces pollution and improves community health outcomes in El Kharga Oasis.

Background

Egypt's recent evolution has many goals — two of which are improving the use of alternative energies and reducing pollution. Egypt uses fossil fuels to generate over 94% of its energy. Fossil fuel combustion produces carbon dioxide, reaching roughly 242 Megatonnes in 2023 (IEA, 2023).

Problem Statement

El Kharga Oasis depends entirely on diesel generators — producing 100% of its estimated 200 kWh/day energy demand (Abdelwahed et al., 2024). Diesel operation elevates PAH concentrations in El Kharga's sediments to 10.18–790.14 ng/g dry weight, causing significant environmental harm and health risks.

Prior Solutions Considered

Zafarana Wind Farm — reliable at 1,400–1,800 GWh/year (Braedt et al., 2022), but prohibitively expensive at $20 million per HAWT unit. Small-scale VAWTs in Mang, Pakistan — clean and sustainable, but suffer from reduced efficiency as only half the turbine captures wind at any moment.

Chosen Solution

A Savonius vertical-axis wind turbine — drag-based, consisting of two curved Benesh-profile blades with 5% overlap for increased pressure differential and higher efficiency. The turbine self-starts at low wind speeds. Guide vanes are added to accelerate and redirect wind toward the concave blade, significantly boosting output.

All components were selected for local availability and structural performance. Below is the full materials list and construction methodology.

Blade Construction

Aluminum sheet laser-cut into two circles (Ø 0.42 m) and two rectangles (0.282×0.8 m). Each rectangle bent to form a Benesh-profile curve: 120° angle, 4.8 cm radius. Steel pipe drilled at 4 points; blade straight sections connected with stud rods, secured by hex nuts and flat washers.

Guide Vane Construction

MDF wood laser-cut into 12 vane blades and two tori. Blades fixed between tori at 60° angles using MDF glue — base model guide vane configuration that accelerates and redirects incoming wind.

Base & Motor Assembly

Two flat wooden pieces drilled — one for pipe diameter, one for ball bearings — fixed to octagonal base with nails. DC generator mounted with a 1:6 gear ratio. Second ball bearing added internally for blade stability after negative result discovery.

Test Plan

Prototype tested at moderate-to-high wind speeds using school air supply at elevation. Generator connected to multimeter. Readings taken every 10 s over 5 min (300 s). Energy (J) = area under the Power-vs-Time graph (P = V × I).

Negative Results & Solutions

MDF Deformation

MDF wood deformed when nails were used to fix it to the torus.

✓ FIX: Switched to MDF glue for all vane attachments.

Hex Nuts Loosening

Hexagonal nuts detached from blades during airborne testing.

✓ FIX: Applied AB glue to all nut connections.

Blade Instability

Blade section shook while rotating due to tilt angle.

✓ FIX: Added third ball bearing for shaft stabilization.

Gear Ratio Challenge

Calculated optimal 1:26 ratio exceeded available rotor torque.

✓ FIX: Experimentally determined 1:7 as optimal, finalized at 1:6.

Drag Force & Pressure Difference

The Savonius rotor uses two Benesh-profile blades. Wind exerts drag force on opposite blade faces: the concave side creates a high-pressure region; the convex (returning) side creates low-pressure. This net pressure difference produces torque and rotation. A Benesh blade profile with overlap ratio 0.10 improves performance by 90.98% over traditional semi-circular design (Kumar & Kumar, 2026). End plates at both rotor ends increase efficiency by 74.9% by reducing flow losses (Wang et al., 2024).

Fd = ½ · Cd · ρ · v² · A   (Eq. 1)
Drag Force — Fd ≈ 7.056 N at V = 6 m/s, Cd ≈ 1, A = swept area
T = Fd × R   (Eq. 2)
Torque — T = 1.4112 N·m at R = 0.2 m (rotor radius). Min self-start speed = 2.4 m/s
Re = ρVD / μ   (Eq. 3)
Reynolds Number — Re = 1.6 × 10⁵ → delayed flow separation → reduced negative torque → +15.2% power coefficient (Sengupta et al., 2025)

Energy Conversion Analysis

The VAWT converts energy in two sequential stages: (1) kinetic wind energy → mechanical rotation via drag force; (2) mechanical rotation → electrical energy via DC generator through electromagnetic induction. The armature rotates in a magnetic field, changing magnetic flux, inducing EMF, rectified to DC via commutator and brush assembly.

Aerodynamic power coefficient of conventional Savonius rotors: Cp = 0.245. Generator conversion losses up to 30% (Shamim et al., 2015). Energy is conserved — losses manifest as heat in armature resistance and bearing friction.

Gear Ratio Analysis

Rotor RPM ≈ 150; Generator rated RPM ≈ 4,000. Mathematically optimal ratio = 1:26 — but the available rotor torque (0.225 N·m) cannot overcome the generator resistance at that ratio. Experimentally tested: 1:5, 1:6, 1:7, 1:8, 1:10, 1:15, 1:20. Ratios 1:15 and 1:20 produced near-zero rotation. Best performance at 1:7.

Gear Ratio = RPMrotor / RPMgenerator   (Eq. 4)
Trotor = 0.225 N·m · Motor torque = 0.032 N·m · Best ratio = 1:7

Guide Vane Analysis

Guide vanes narrow the wind path into the concave blade, increasing velocity via the continuity equation. Since power ∝ v³, even a small velocity increase yields dramatic power gain. Additionally, vanes redirect wind to hit the concave blade exclusively and block the convex blade — reducing negative torque and increasing net rotation (Ahmad, 2015).

A₁V₁ = A₂V₂   (Eq. 7 — Continuity)
Velocity increase ≈ 2.5 m/s through guide vanes (6 m/s → 8.35 m/s)
P = ½ · ρ · A · v³ · Cp   (Eq. 8 — Power Output)
Power ratio: (8.35³ − 6³) / 6³ = 1.69 → guide vanes cause >69% power increase

Real-Life Application — El Kharga Oasis

The project is targeted for implementation in El Kharga Oasis — 200 kWh/day electricity demand, 100% from diesel generators. Wind speeds range between 6.8–7.9 m/s, sufficient to power Savonius VAWTs effectively. Installation would reduce fossil fuel dependency, lower CO₂ emissions, and generate economic savings from reduced diesel costs. Inaction risks continued health damage, environmental degradation, and economic instability.

Maglev Dynamo

Replacing the mechanical bearing with a magnetic bearing uses opposing magnetic poles to create levitation — reducing vibration by 30%, reducing shaft-dynamo friction to near zero, and increasing output power considerably (Aravind et al., 2012). Maglev generator efficiency: approximately 96% (Sam, 2025). Not used in prototype due to high cost and volume — recommended for real-life deployment.

Glass Fiber Instead of Aluminium

Glass fiber (GFRP) is lighter than aluminum, reducing rotational inertia and enabling self-starting at even lower wind speeds. GFRP can be molded into complex 3D blade profiles without joints or welds, producing smoother aerodynamic surfaces with high strength, wear/corrosion resistance, low density, and environmental stability (Varun K S, 2024). Finite Element Analysis confirms composite materials allow multi-objective optimization of stress, deformation, and weight for Savonius blades (Yazik et al., 2024).
